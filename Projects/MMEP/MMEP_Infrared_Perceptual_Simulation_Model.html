MMEP Infrared Perceptual Simulation Model


Purpose of This Simulation

  • Answer a scientific question rather than prove superiority
  • Determine whether structured modulation plus computation yields different or additional perceptual information than conventional IR optics
  • Treat success as learning, regardless of outcome
  • Validate or falsify assumptions in a controlled, simplified environment
  • Inform future design decisions without committing to hardware

Guiding Principle

  • The goal is not to make a better system
  • The goal is to understand the information consequences of structured modulation
  • Winning condition is increased understanding, not performance dominance
  • Simulation is a tool for insight, not validation bias

Scope and Abstractions

Dimensionality

  • Simulation is strictly two-dimensional
  • X-axis represents lateral spatial dimension
  • Z-axis represents propagation direction
  • 2D model represents a slice of a 3D system

What Is Modeled

  • Infrared wave propagation in 2D
  • A modulated aperture represented as a line
  • Time-varying phase or amplitude modulation
  • Simple infrared-emitting scenes
  • Sensor plane intensity measurements
  • Noise and resolution limits

What Is Explicitly Ignored

  • Full electromagnetic boundary conditions
  • Polarization effects
  • 3D scattering and occlusion
  • Mechanical constraints
  • Manufacturing tolerances
  • Atmospheric absorption details

Infrared Regime Definition

  • Simulation may operate in:
    • near-infrared (approx. 1–2 microns)
    • or thermal infrared (approx. 8–12 microns)
  • Wavelength treated as a controllable parameter
  • Multiple wavelengths may be tested for comparison
  • Scene emission treated as incoherent infrared sources

Baseline System: Conventional Infrared Optics

Baseline Assumptions

  • Static aperture or lens
  • Fixed point-spread function (PSF)
  • Single observation produces single image
  • Resolution limited by diffraction and aperture size
  • No temporal diversity

Baseline Measurement Model

  • Scene intensity distribution
  • Convolution with PSF
  • Addition of noise
  • Resulting image is the baseline output

Baseline Metrics

  • Spatial resolution
  • Contrast
  • Noise sensitivity
  • Ambiguity in closely spaced emitters

Proposed System: Structured Modulated Infrared Sensing

Modulator Representation

  • A single straight line representing the modulator surface
  • Line contains multiple dimples or micro-features
  • Each dimple modeled as a localized phase or amplitude perturbation
  • Dimples staggered along the line

Emulating 3D Rotation in 2D

  • Time-varying modulation pattern
  • Patterns shift, morph, or slide along the line
  • Each time step represents a different effective slice of a rotating 3D surface
  • Multiple modulation states sampled sequentially

Modulation Parameters

  • Dimple depth
  • Dimple width
  • Dimple spacing
  • Modulation speed
  • Number of modulation states
  • Wavelength-dependent phase response

Measurement Process

  • For each modulation state:
    • apply modulator to incoming or outgoing IR field
    • propagate field to sensor plane
    • record intensity image
  • Result is a time series of coded IR images
  • Each individual image may be low quality
  • Information is distributed across the set

Reconstruction and Analysis

Reconstruction Goals

  • Estimate scene intensity distribution
  • Reduce ambiguity present in single-shot imaging
  • Assess whether multiple coded observations improve inference

Reconstruction Methods (Conceptual)

  • Correlation-based backprojection
  • Simple inverse filtering
  • Iterative refinement
  • Comparison against known ground truth

Fusion Across Time

  • Combine multiple modulation states
  • Evaluate diminishing returns with increasing samples
  • Examine sensitivity to noise and model mismatch

Comparison Methodology

  • Use identical scenes for baseline and modulated system
  • Use identical noise levels and photon budgets
  • Compare:
    • single baseline image
    • reconstructed image from multiple modulated observations

Evaluation Metrics

  • Ability to resolve closely spaced IR sources
  • Reduction in uncertainty over time
  • Robustness to noise
  • Stability of reconstruction
  • Sensitivity to modulation parameters

Multi-Wavelength Extension (Optional)

  • Simulate two or more IR wavelengths
  • Apply same modulation geometry
  • Observe wavelength-dependent differences
  • Fuse multi-band results
  • Evaluate conditioning and ambiguity reduction

Expected Outcomes

  • Possible improvement in effective resolution
  • Possible no improvement
  • Possible tradeoffs between complexity and gain
  • Clear understanding of where modulation helps or does not help

Interpretation Rules

  • A negative result is still a valid and valuable outcome
  • Results inform architecture decisions
  • Simulation outcomes guide whether physical prototyping is justified
  • No conclusion assumed in advance

Relationship to Broader MMEP Architecture

  • IR simulation is one modality test
  • Same framework can extend to RF, acoustic, or optical regimes
  • Confirms whether structured modulation is a generalizable principle

Next Steps After Simulation

  1. Analyze results objectively
  2. Document findings and limitations
  3. Decide whether to refine simulation or change assumptions
  4. Determine whether a minimal physical experiment is warranted
  5. Integrate insights into overall MMEP platform design
