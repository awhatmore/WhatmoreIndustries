Multi-Modal Structured Sensing and Computational Perception Platform


Vision and Motivation

  • Create a global perception platform that maintains situational truth when humans and traditional systems fail
  • Focus on awareness, understanding, and continuity rather than action or force
  • Enable long-duration observation without fatigue
  • Design a reusable platform architecture rather than a single-purpose device
  • Combine physics-based sensing with AI-driven reconstruction

Core Problem Being Addressed

  • Human perception fails in:
    • darkness
    • smoke, fog, dust
    • cluttered or complex environments
    • hostile or denied areas
    • extended-duration incidents
  • Traditional sensors fail when:
    • relying on single modality
    • requiring constant human attention
    • needing line-of-sight only
  • Long-duration threats cause:
    • responder stand-off
    • loss of situational awareness
    • breakdown of coordinated response
  • Information denial is the primary failure mode

Foundational Insight

  • Change over time is the most reliable signal
  • Static snapshots are insufficient
  • Multiple weak signals combined outperform one strong sensor
  • Hardware limitations can be offset by computational methods
  • Modulation plus reconstruction increases effective resolution

Structured Modulation Concept

High-Level Description

  • A broad beam (RF, optical, sub-IR, acoustic) is emitted or received
  • A modulating element is placed between source and environment
  • The modulator alters phase, angle, intensity, or scattering
  • Multiple configurations are sampled over time
  • Each configuration produces a unique reflection pattern
  • AI fuses patterns into a higher-information representation

Dimpled Surface / Modulated Aperture

  • Surface contains many dimples or micro-features
  • Each dimple acts as:
    • a phase shifter
    • a micro-scatterer
    • a directional modifier
  • Rotation or actuation changes the aggregate pattern
  • Creates phase diversity and view diversity
  • Acts as a synthetic aperture over time

Intuitive Analogy

  • Flashlight shining through patterned glass
  • Rotating the glass produces different light patterns
  • Reflected patterns encode spatial information
  • AI reconstructs scene properties from correlations

Computational Imaging Principles

  • Coded aperture imaging
  • Synthetic aperture reconstruction
  • Structured illumination
  • Phase-diverse sampling
  • Metasurface-inspired modulation
  • Reconstruction via correlation and inference rather than direct imaging

RF Sensing Layer (High-Level)

  • RF reflects strongly from conductive and metallic materials
  • RF captures:
    • motion via Doppler
    • phase shifts
    • orientation changes
  • RF can operate in:
    • darkness
    • smoke
    • partial occlusion
  • Resolution normally limited by wavelength and antenna size

Dimpled RF Antenna Effects

  • Modulated surface introduces phase diversity
  • Rotation simulates larger effective aperture
  • Improves angular resolution
  • Enhances direction finding
  • Enables better separation of reflective sources

Optical and Sub-IR Layer

  • Uses visible, near-IR, or sub-IR light
  • Lasers or structured illumination preferred
  • Provides:
    • high spatial resolution
    • surface detail
    • reflectivity contrast
  • Limited to line-of-sight
  • Sensitive to fog, smoke, dust

Structured Optical Modulation

  • Patterned optics or surfaces shape the beam
  • Multiple illumination patterns generated
  • Each pattern produces distinct reflection
  • AI reconstructs surface geometry and material hints

Thermal Sensing Layer

  • Detects heat signatures
  • Confirms presence of humans or active systems
  • Works in darkness
  • Highlights:
    • body heat
    • recently handled objects
    • heat leaks from small openings
  • Low spatial resolution compared to optics

Multi-Modal Sensor Fusion

  • Combine RF, thermal, IR, and RGB
  • AI aligns data temporally and spatially
  • Confidence increases when modalities correlate
  • Focus on patterns, not object identity
  • Example correlation:
    • thermal human signature
    • RF reflective motion near hands
    • optical limb movement

Change Detection Strategy

  • Initial baseline capture
  • Establish normal state for each location
  • Continuous comparison against baseline
  • Trigger escalation on:
    • motion
    • thermal anomalies
    • reflective pattern shifts
  • Increase sampling rate on suspicious zones

Event-Driven Observation Model

  • Default low-power monitoring
  • Rotate sensing focus across zones
  • Escalate attention when changes occur
  • De-escalate when stability returns
  • Designed for long-duration operation

Acoustic and Directional Sensing (Conceptual)

  • Detect impulsive or anomalous sound events
  • Multiple nodes estimate direction
  • Correlate sound events with other sensor data
  • Use timing and intensity differences
  • Provide coarse localization, not identification

Extended-Duration Threat Awareness

  • Addresses multi-hour or multi-site incidents
  • Maintains perception when humans cannot enter
  • Reduces uncertainty over time
  • Supports informed decision-making
  • Prevents total information blackout

Safety and Ethical Framing

  • System provides information only
  • No autonomous action or targeting
  • Focus on awareness and risk assessment
  • Human decision-makers remain in control
  • Designed for civilian, industrial, and humanitarian use
