Phase Change Octave Mapping


Project Overview

  • Identify repeating octave-like base-12 patterns in phase-change behavior
  • Begin with simple atomic systems and progress toward complex systems
  • Observe effects of temperature, pressure, and atomic structure

Core Hypothesis

  • Phase changes follow a uniform and mappable structure using a base-12 framework
  • Complexity emerges from simpler atomic and electronic interactions
  • Deviations from expected patterns indicate deeper structural mechanisms

Foundational Layer


Hydrogen

  • Single proton
  • Single electron
  • Simplest atomic system
  • Establishes baseline phase-change behavior

Helium

  • Two protons
  • Two neutrons
  • Two electrons
  • Introduces electron pairing effects
  • Serves as first complexity comparison against hydrogen

Increasing Atomic Complexity

  • Higher elements introduce complex electron shell configurations
  • Phase-change behavior becomes layered but remains related
  • Doubles, triples, and sixes dominate structural patterns
  • Fives and sevens may appear as irregular edge cases

Molecular Complexity


Water

  • Two hydrogen atoms
  • One oxygen atom
  • Multiple interaction layers
  • Solid, liquid, and vapor phases represent higher-order behavior

Environmental Variables

  • Temperature is the primary phase-change driver
  • Pressure modifies phase boundaries
  • Combined variables increase system complexity

Analytical Framework

  • Base-12 logarithmic mapping
  • Octave-style pattern recognition
  • Visual and behavioral correlation across systems

Next Steps

  1. Log hydrogen phase-change behavior
  2. Log helium phase-change behavior
  3. Compare hydrogen and helium patterns
  4. Extend mapping to higher atomic elements
  5. Introduce molecular systems
